Modeling Continuous Distributions in Real-World Data: A Statistical Analysis Using Probability Density Functions

Introduction

Continuous probability models allow analysts to describe the behavior of real-world measurements, from household income to environmental sensor readings, using mathematical functions whose area under the curve equals exactly one. The normal distribution, first formalized by Gauss in the early nineteenth century, remains the most widely encountered continuous model because many natural and economic processes exhibit symmetric, bell-shaped variation around a mean (Blitzstein & Hwang, 2019). Yet real data are rarely perfectly normal: empirical histograms often show skewness, bounded support, or step-like structure that calls for alternative models such as uniform, triangular, or piecewise-uniform distributions (DeGroot & Schervish, 2012).

This report uses three simulated CSV datasets provided for Grade 10 to compare the fit of five continuous probability models — uniform, triangular, linear, piecewise uniform, and normal — and draws conclusions about which model best describes each dataset.

Research Question

Which continuous probability distribution — uniform, triangular, linear, piecewise uniform, or normal — best fits each of the three provided Grade 10 datasets, and how do the fitted models compare in terms of probability calculations for intervals centered on the mean?

Methodology

Each dataset was uploaded to the C9 Data Modeling tab of this webpage. For each file, the webpage automatically computed the sample mean (x̄) and standard deviation (s) as moment estimates. The following distributions were then fitted:

  1. Uniform: parameters â = min(data), b̂ = max(data); f(x) = 1/(b−a).
  2. Triangular: â = min, b̂ = max, ĉ = mode (bin midpoint with highest frequency).
  3. Linear (increasing): f(x) = 2(x−a)/(b−a)², x ∈ [a,b].
  4. Piecewise uniform: median split; each half assigned a constant height so its area is 0.5.
  5. Normal: μ̂ = x̄, σ̂ = s.

For each fitted model, the following probabilities were calculated using numerical integration (200-point Riemann sum) directly on the fitted PDF:

  • P(μ − σ < X < μ + σ) — probability within one standard deviation of the mean.
  • P(X < μ − 2σ) + P(X > μ + 2σ) — probability in both tails beyond two standard deviations.

These two quantities were chosen because they capture how concentrated the distribution is near its center and how heavy its tails are — key features for comparing models (NIST/SEMATECH, 2012).

Results

After uploading each CSV file in the C9 tab, the following observations were made from the histogram–PDF overlays:

Dataset 1 showed an approximately bell-shaped histogram, suggesting a normal fit. The normal PDF overlay aligned closely with the histogram peaks. For the normal model, P(μ−σ < X < μ+σ) ≈ 0.683, consistent with the 68–95–99.7 empirical rule (Blitzstein & Hwang, 2019). The piecewise-uniform and uniform models produced flat PDFs that underrepresented the central peak.

Dataset 2 appeared roughly uniform across its range, with no pronounced central peak. The uniform and piecewise-uniform models therefore achieved the best visual fit. The normal model over-concentrated probability at the center.

Dataset 3 showed mild right-skew (longer upper tail). The triangular distribution — with mode shifted toward the lower end — or the increasing-linear model captured this better than the symmetric normal model.

Model Comparison

Based on the histogram–PDF overlays generated in the C9 tab:

  • The normal distribution is most appropriate for symmetric, unimodal datasets (Dataset 1). Its strength is that it is fully characterized by two parameters; its limitation is that it assigns non-zero probability to values outside any real physical constraint.
  • The uniform distribution is most appropriate when all values in a range are equally likely (Dataset 2). Its strength is simplicity; its limitation is that it cannot capture any concentration or skew.
  • The triangular distribution is most appropriate for data with a clear mode and bounded support (Dataset 3). Its strength is intuitive parameterization; its limitation is the sharp corners at the endpoints.
  • The linear distribution captures monotone increase or decrease across a bounded interval but cannot model symmetric data without modification.
  • The piecewise-uniform distribution offers flexibility by allowing different density levels across sub-intervals, but the exact split point must be chosen carefully.

Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates that no single continuous distribution is universally best. Dataset 1 was best described by the normal model, Dataset 2 by the uniform model, and Dataset 3 by the triangular or linear-increasing model. In practice, model selection should combine visual inspection of histogram–PDF overlays with formal goodness-of-fit metrics such as the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test (DeGroot & Schervish, 2012). The interactive webpage built for this project makes it possible to compare all five models simultaneously by switching between options in the C9 tab, providing an accessible tool for exploratory data analysis in educational and applied contexts.
